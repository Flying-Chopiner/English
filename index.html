Introduction

This is a purely personal strategy guide for the Postgraduate Entrance Examination English Test I, encompassing four sections: cloze tests, reading comprehension, new question types, and translation. The purpose of this guide is to explore a method akin to exploiting a 'speedrun bug' in a game for quickly navigating through the actual English test questions. It demands a modest vocabulary level—at least a high school reserve—to enhance comfort during the exercise.

No specific grammar expertise is required, but understanding a few key points is crucial:

1) In conversations, the focus of a foreigner's speech is often either at the beginning or the end. For instance, at the outset, I clarified that the subject here is the strategy guide, which is positioned at the beginning. When I mention there are several points, the emphasis is likely to follow—this is self-explanatory.

2) Argumentative sentences are the soul of an article, but you must discern between argumentation and description. "You are handsome" is descriptive; exaggeration is permissible here. "I am handsome" is a conclusion, devoid of exaggeration—it simply conveys a fact. Learning to differentiate these is essential.

3) Emotional judgment: when someone says, "You are nice, but, XXX," it implies a negative. You must learn to combine emotional words with conjunctions to make judgments. Also, note the use of progressive and coordinate structures, as in "You are good, you are very good, but there's better." Hence, the conjunctions and sentence relationships taught in elementary school Chinese may be surprisingly crucial here.
